    \begin{block}{Introduction}
      \begin{itemize}
        \item LLM confidence is of significant interest in addition to accuracy.
        \item Calibration is usually reported as a scalar (typically Expected
          Calibration Error, ECE), possibly obscuring underlying structure:
      \end{itemize}
\[
  \mathrm{ECE} = \sum_b \frac{|B_b|}{n}\,|\mathrm{acc}(B_b) - \mathrm{conf}(B_b)|
\]
      \textbf{Questions:}
      \begin{itemize}
        \item How does calibration vary across QA domains?
        \item Is this variation consistent across models?
        \item How is calibration associated with other features (scale,
          question-level covariates)?
      \end{itemize}

      % LLMs are increasingly deployed in high-stakes settings---healthcare,
      % law, education---where the reliability of model-expressed confidence
      % matters as much as accuracy. Yet calibration is nearly always reported
      % as a single \emph{aggregate scalar}, obscuring whether miscalibration is
      % diffuse or concentrated in specific knowledge domains.

      % \vspace{0.4em}
      % We import the logic of a rigorous \emph{fairness audit} into calibration
      % evaluation and ask three questions:
      % \begin{itemize}
      %   \item \textbf{Where} is calibration good and bad? (variance
      %     decomposition; FDR-corrected subject rankings)
      %   \item \textbf{How consistent} is the answer across models?
      %     (cross-model rank correlations)
      %   \item \textbf{What mechanisms} drive the heterogeneity?
      %     (question-level predictors; a within-Qwen scaling sweep)
      % \end{itemize}

      % \vspace{0.4em}
      % \textbf{Why it matters.} If miscalibration is diffuse, a single global
      % fix (e.g.\ temperature scaling) suffices. If it is concentrated in
      % subject clusters that are \emph{stable across models}, subject-aware
      % recalibration becomes the structurally appropriate scoping choice.
    \end{block}

    \begin{block}{Data \& Methods}
      \textbf{Data.} 
      Questions: MMLU: 14{,}042 four-option questions across 57 subjects
      nested in 4 domains (STEM, Social Sciences, Humanities, Other). Subject
      sizes 100--1{,}534 (median 173).

      Confidence elicitation: renormalized softmax over the four answer tokens:
\[
  c_i = \max_{a \in \{A,B,C,D\}} \frac{e^{\ell_a}}{\sum_{a'} e^{\ell_{a'}}}
\]

      \textbf{Models:} GPT-4o-mini,
      Llama-3-8B-Instruct, and Qwen-2.5-\{0.5B, 1.5B, 3B, 7B, 14B\}-Instruct.
      The outcome is the signed \emph{calibration gap}
      $\mathrm{gap}_i = c_i - y_i$, with $y_i \in \{0,1\}$ indicating
      correctness (positive under overconfidence).

      \textbf{Three-level mixed model} (questions $\to$ subjects $\to$ domains):
      \[
        \mathrm{gap}_{ijk} = \mu + \mathbf{x}_{ijk}^\top\boldsymbol{\beta}
          + u_k + v_{jk} + \varepsilon_{ijk},
      \]
      fit using REML.

      \vspace{0.4em}
      \textbf{Also:} per-subject one-sample $t$-tests with Benjamini--Hochberg FDR control at
      $\alpha=0.05$; cross-model Spearman correlations of subject-level ECE
      with bootstrap CIs.
    \end{block}

    \begin{block}{Results}
      \textbf{Every model is overconfident.} The mean calibration gap (mixed-model
      intercept) is positive for all seven models, ranging $0.18$--$0.31$. This is consistent with prior work
      (Guo et al.\ 2017; Kadavath et al.\ 2022). 

      \vspace{0.5em}
      \textbf{Calibration across subjects}
      Across the six larger models, \texttt{virology} and
      \texttt{professional\_law} appear in the top-10 most miscalibrated for
      every model; a cluster of college-level technical subjects
      (\texttt{abstract\_algebra}, \texttt{college\_mathematics},
      \texttt{machine\_learning}) is in the top-5 for four of six.
      Symmetrically, \texttt{marketing} and \texttt{high\_school\_psychology}
      are best-calibrated for every model. Specialization level tracks
      miscalibration: high-school subjects show systematically smaller gaps
      than their college-level counterparts.

    \begin{block}{FDR-controlled miscalibration testing}
      For each of the 57 subjects $s$ we test whether its mean calibration gap
      is zero,
      \[
        H_0:\ \mathbb{E}\!\left[\mathrm{gap}_i \mid \text{subject } s\right] = 0
        \quad\text{vs.}\quad
        H_1:\ \mathbb{E}\!\left[\mathrm{gap}_i \mid \text{subject } s\right] \neq 0,
      \]
      with a two-sided one-sample $t$-test, controlling the false discovery
      rate (FDR) at $\alpha=0.05$ via Benjamini--Hochberg (1995). Rejections are
      near-universal (the five largest models reject all 57 subjects), so the
      story is the \emph{magnitude} of the gaps, not the binary outcome:
      best- to worst-calibrated gap ranges span $3.8$--$13.0\times$ across the
      six larger models.
    \end{block}

    \begin{block}{Robustness Checks}
      ECE rankings are stable across bin counts (Spearman $\geq 0.97$ vs.\ the
      20-bin baseline) and agree with a strictly proper scoring rule, the
      Negative Log Calibration Score (Spearman $\geq 0.79$); two- and
      three-level ICC estimates are numerically identical.
      % (John): one of the reviewers asked about Brier score, we could maybe include this here. However, mentioning NLCS addresses this concern pretty well already. 
    \end{block}

    \begin{block}{Cross-model \& cross-family stability}
      All 15 pairwise Spearman correlations among the six larger models fall in
      $[0.80, 0.94]$. Cross-family GPT-4o-mini vs.\ Qwen-7B/14B ($0.92/0.91$) is
      as strong as the best within-family pair (Qwen-7B vs.\ 14B, $0.94$).
      Qwen-0.5B is a conspicuous exception ($\rho \in [0.13, 0.20]$), driven by
      $10.6\times$ positional bias toward D---a floor below which our
      elicitation protocol breaks down.

      \end{center}
      {\small Spearman rank correlation of subject-level ECE across the seven
      models (57 common subjects).}
    \end{block}


    \begin{block}{Question-level predictors}
      The three-level mixed model relates the calibration gap to standardized
      question features. Word count positively predicts overconfidence for
      GPT-4o-mini and the larger Qwens; negation and option length are mostly
      inert. The most informative pattern is the \textbf{entropy} coefficient
      (next panel).

      \begin{center}
        \includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{coef_plot_7m.pdf}

      calibration-gap scale. The entropy coefficient shows a clear sign
      progression across the Qwen scaling sweep.}
    \end{block}

    \begin{block}{Difficulty, entropy, and scale}
      Entropy $H_i$ of the A/B/C/D distribution proxies how hard the model finds
      an item. Naively, higher entropy should shrink the gap (top-1 confidence is
      mechanically lower). Instead, within the matched-precision Qwen sweep
      (1.5B fp16, 3B, 7B, 14B) the entropy coefficient grows monotonically:
      \[
        -0.004 \to +0.006 \to +0.029 \to +0.040.
      \]
      At 7B/14B, confidence stays near saturation across
      the first three entropy quartiles while accuracy keeps dropping, so the
      gap widens on the model's hardest items (Q1 vs.\ Q4 gap: $+0.05$ vs.\
      $+0.37$ at 7B; $+0.03$ vs.\ $+0.46$ at 14B). The between-subject ICC also
      jumps from 1.5B to 3B ($0.017 \to 0.051$) and is flat thereafter---
      consistent with RLHF-induced confidence saturation (Kadavath et al.\ 2022;
      Nakkiran et al.\ 2025).

      \begin{center}
        \includegraphics[width=0.92\linewidth]{entropy_mechanism_7m.pdf}
      \end{center}
      {\small Confidence (solid) and accuracy (dashed) by entropy quartile;
      shaded area is the calibration gap.}
    \end{block}

    \begin{block}{Conclusion}
      Subject-level miscalibration is primarily a property of the {task},
      not of any single training pipeline: it is large and stable across LLM
      families above ${\sim}1$B parameters (all 15 larger-model pairs
      $\rho \in [0.80, 0.94]$). Applied/factual high-school subjects are reliably
      well-calibrated; college-level quantitative and niche professional content
      are reliably bad. This cross-model stability is the precondition that makes
      {subject-aware} recalibration potentially worthwhile a global fix would
      under-correct the worst cluster (\texttt{professional\_law},
      \texttt{virology}, college-level technical content) while over-correcting
      subjects like \texttt{marketing}. 
    \end{block}

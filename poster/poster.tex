    \begin{block}{Motivation}
      \begin{itemize}
        \item Calibration is usually reported as one aggregate scalar (ECE),
          which can hide \emph{where} miscalibration concentrates:
      \end{itemize}
\[
  \mathrm{ECE} = \sum_b \frac{|B_b|}{n}\,|\mathrm{acc}(B_b) - \mathrm{conf}(B_b)|
\]
      \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{The analogy:} one accuracy number across demographic
          groups would not pass as a fairness audit --- the same logic
          applies to calibration.
        \vspace{0.3em}
        \item \textbf{Where} is calibration good and bad, and how much?
        \item \textbf{Is that pattern consistent} across models?
        \item \textbf{What drives it} (question features, model scale)?
      \end{itemize}
    \end{block}

    \begin{block}{Data \& Methods}
      \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Data:} MMLU --- 14{,}042 four-option questions across
          57 subjects nested in 4 domains (STEM, Social Sciences,
          Humanities, Other); subject sizes 100--1{,}534 (median 173).
        \vspace{0.3em}
        \item \textbf{Confidence:} renormalized softmax over the four
          answer tokens:
\[
  c_i = \max_{a \in \{A,B,C,D\}} \frac{e^{\ell_a}}{\sum_{a'} e^{\ell_{a'}}}
\]
        \item \textbf{Models:} GPT-4o-mini, Llama-3-8B-Instruct, and
          Qwen-2.5-\{0.5B, 1.5B, 3B, 7B, 14B\}-Instruct. Outcome is the
          signed \emph{calibration gap} $\mathrm{gap}_i = c_i - y_i$,
          $y_i \in \{0,1\}$ (positive under overconfidence).
        \vspace{0.3em}
        \item \textbf{Model:} three-level mixed model (questions $\to$
          subjects $\to$ domains):
\[
  \mathrm{gap}_{ijk} = \mu + \mathbf{x}_{ijk}^\top\boldsymbol{\beta}
    + u_k + v_{jk} + \varepsilon_{ijk},
\]
          fit via REML.
        \item \textbf{Testing:} per-subject one-sample $t$-tests with
          Benjamini--Hochberg FDR control at $\alpha=0.05$; cross-model
          Spearman correlations of subject-level ECE with bootstrap CIs.
      \end{itemize}
    \end{block}

    \begin{block}{Structure Exists, But Is Modest}
      \begin{itemize}
        \item Most calibration-gap variance is at the question level
          (92.0--98.5\%); domain variance collapses to zero.
        \item Between-subject ICC spans nearly $5\times$ across models ---
          small in absolute terms\ldots
        \item \ldots but (next panel) large enough to organize a
          $3.8$--$13.0\times$ range in subject-level miscalibration.
      \end{itemize}

      \vspace{0.5em}

    \begin{block}{Where Calibration Fails}
      \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Every model is overconfident on average:} intercept
          positive for all seven models, $0.18$--$0.31$ (Guo et al.\ 2017;
          Kadavath et al.\ 2022).
        \vspace{0.3em}
        \item \textbf{But the failure is not uniform.} \texttt{virology} and
          \texttt{professional\_law}: top-10 worst-calibrated for every
          model above 1B parameters.
        \item College-level technical cluster (\texttt{abstract\_algebra},
          \texttt{college\_mathematics}, \texttt{machine\_learning}): top-5
          for four of six.
        \item \texttt{marketing} and \texttt{high\_school\_psychology}:
          best-calibrated for every model.
        \item Specialization level tracks miscalibration: high-school
          subjects show systematically smaller gaps than their
          college-level counterparts.
      \end{itemize}

    \begin{block}{Magnitude, Not the Binary Count}
      For each of the 57 subjects $s$ we test whether its mean calibration gap
      is zero,
      \[
        H_0:\ \mathbb{E}\!\left[\mathrm{gap}_i \mid \text{subject } s\right] = 0
        \quad\text{vs.}\quad
        H_1:\ \mathbb{E}\!\left[\mathrm{gap}_i \mid \text{subject } s\right] \neq 0,
      \]
      via a two-sided one-sample $t$-test, controlling FDR at $\alpha=0.05$
      via Benjamini--Hochberg (1995).
      \begin{itemize}
        \item Rejections are near-universal (the five largest models reject
          all 57 subjects) --- with this much data and a large global
          overconfidence effect, that's expected, \emph{not informative}.
        \vspace{0.3em}
        \item The real finding is the \emph{magnitude}: best- to
          worst-calibrated gap ranges span $3.8$--$13.0\times$ across the
          six larger models.
        \item The \emph{same} subjects sit at each extreme, every time.
        \vspace{0.3em}
        \item \textbf{Robustness:} ECE rankings are stable across bin counts
          (Spearman $\geq 0.97$ vs.\ the 20-bin baseline) and agree with a
          strictly proper scoring rule, the Negative Log Calibration Score
          (Spearman $\geq 0.79$); 2- and 3-level ICC estimates match.
      \end{itemize}
    \end{block}

    \begin{block}{Stable Across Models and Families}
      \begin{itemize}
        \item All 15 pairwise Spearman correlations among the six larger
          models fall in $[0.80, 0.94]$.
        \item Cross-family GPT-4o-mini vs.\ Qwen-7B/14B ($0.92/0.91$) is as
          strong as the best within-family pair (Qwen-7B vs.\ 14B, $0.94$).
        \vspace{0.3em}
        \item The same subjects are hard \emph{regardless of architecture or
          scale}.
        \vspace{0.3em}
        \item Qwen-0.5B is a conspicuous exception ($\rho \in [0.13, 0.20]$),
          driven by $10.6\times$ positional bias toward D --- a floor below
          which our elicitation protocol breaks down.
      \end{itemize}

      \end{center}
      {\small Spearman rank correlation of subject-level ECE across the seven

    \end{block}

    \begin{block}{What Predicts the Gap}
      \begin{itemize}
        \item Word count positively predicts overconfidence for
          GPT-4o-mini and the larger Qwens.
        \item Negation and option length: mostly inert.
        \vspace{0.3em}
        \item Most informative pattern: the \textbf{entropy} coefficient
          (next panel).
      \end{itemize}

    \begin{block}{The Gap Grows With Scale}
      \begin{itemize}
        \item Entropy $H_i$ of the A/B/C/D distribution proxies item
          difficulty. Naively, higher entropy should \emph{shrink} the gap
          (top-1 confidence is mechanically lower).
        \vspace{0.3em}
        \item Instead, within the matched-precision Qwen sweep (1.5B fp16,
          3B, 7B, 14B), the entropy coefficient grows monotonically:
\[
  -0.004 \to +0.006 \to +0.029 \to +0.040.
\]
        \item At 7B/14B, confidence stays near saturation across the first
          three entropy quartiles while accuracy keeps dropping, so the
          gap widens on the model's hardest items (Q1 vs.\ Q4 gap: $+0.05$
          vs.\ $+0.37$ at 7B; $+0.03$ vs.\ $+0.46$ at 14B).
        \vspace{0.3em}
        \item Between-subject ICC also jumps from 1.5B to 3B
          ($0.017 \to 0.051$) and is flat thereafter --- consistent with
          RLHF-induced confidence saturation (Kadavath et al.\ 2022;
          Nakkiran et al.\ 2025).
      \end{itemize}

      \vspace{0.4em}
      \begin{center}
        \includegraphics[width=0.90\linewidth]{entropy_mechanism_7m.pdf}
      \end{center}
      {\small Confidence (solid) and accuracy (dashed) by entropy quartile;
      shaded area is the calibration gap.}
    \end{block}

    \begin{alertblock}{Why Subject-Level, Not Model-Level}
      \begin{itemize}
        \item A single global fix (e.g.\ temperature scaling) cannot work
          here: one scalar $T$ tuned for the worst subjects
          (\texttt{professional\_law}, \texttt{virology}, gap up to
          $0.487$) would badly overcorrect well-calibrated subjects like
          \texttt{marketing} (gap $0.037$) --- the $13\times$ spread is too
          wide for any single correction.
        \vspace{0.3em}
        \item Because the same subjects are hard \emph{across every model
          family and scale we tested}, this is a property of the
          \emph{task}, not a training-pipeline quirk --- so the fix has to
          live at the subject level too.
        \vspace{0.3em}
        \item \textbf{Practical upshot:} the recurring worst-calibrated
          subjects double as a portable pre-deployment checklist --- flag
          likely miscalibration on a \emph{new} model without re-running the
          full 57-subject audit.
      \end{itemize}
    \end{alertblock}

    \begin{block}{Takeaways}
      \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{The unit of analysis is the subject, not the model.}
          Aggregate ECE hides a $3.8$--$13.0\times$ range in miscalibration
          magnitude that a single scalar cannot see.
        \vspace{0.3em}
        \item \textbf{Miscalibration is task-driven.} Subject rankings are
          stable across three families and six scales
          ($\rho \in [0.80,0.94]$) --- model-level retraining is unlikely to
          resolve it.
        \vspace{0.3em}
        \item \textbf{Open question:} \emph{why} these subjects are hardest
          (abstractness, training-data rarity, or something else) remains
          unresolved.
      \end{itemize}
    \end{block}

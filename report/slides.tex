% ============================================================
%  1. MOTIVATION
% ============================================================

\begin{frame}{What is calibration — and why does it matter?}
A model is \textbf{well-calibrated} if its stated confidence tracks empirical accuracy:
\begin{itemize}
  \item 80\% confident $\Rightarrow$ correct 80\% of the time
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.5em}
Miscalibration is typically reported as a \textbf{single number} (Expected Calibration Error):
\[
  \mathrm{ECE} = \sum_b \frac{|B_b|}{n}\,|\text{acc}(B_b) - \text{conf}(B_b)|
\]

\vspace{0.5em}
\textbf{The problem:} aggregate ECE over 57 subjects, 4 domains, 14{,}042 questions
hides \emph{where} and \emph{how much} miscalibration occurs.

\vspace{0.5em}
\textbf{Analogy:} reporting one accuracy number across demographic groups is
insufficient for a fairness audit. The same logic applies to calibration.
\end{frame}
\note{
  \textbf{[~45 sec]}

  Open with the definition — make sure the audience knows what calibration means
  before anything else. The formula is on the slide so you don't need to dwell on it;
  just say ``ECE is the weighted average gap between confidence and accuracy across
  confidence bins.''

  The key move is the fairness analogy: just like we'd never accept a single accuracy
  number as a fairness audit, a single ECE number is not a calibration audit.
  Land that point before moving on — it's the whole motivation.

  If anyone in the room works on ML deployment, you can add: ``when a model says
  it's 90\% confident, users treat that as a reliability signal — if that signal is
  only accurate on average, users in certain domains are being systematically misled.''
}

% ------------------------------------------------------------

\begin{frame}{Research questions}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{How much} of calibration variance is attributable to domain vs.\ subject
    vs.\ individual question?
  \item \textbf{What question features} predict the direction and magnitude of
    miscalibration?
  \item \textbf{Are patterns consistent} across models, or model-specific?
\end{enumerate}

\vspace{1em}
\begin{block}{Framework}
  Three-level mixed model + isotonic calibration curves + empirical Bayes shrinkage
  + Benjamini--Hochberg FDR correction
\end{block}
\end{frame}
\note{
  \textbf{[~30 sec]}

  Read the three questions quickly — the audience will follow.
  For the framework block: ``I won't spend time on each method now; the key idea is
  that we want statistically rigorous answers, not just descriptive bar charts.''

  Don't explain what BH correction is here — that comes up naturally in the
  robustness slide. Just name-drop it so the audience knows it's coming.
}

\begin{frame}{Data \& confidence elicitation}
\textbf{MMLU}: 14{,}042 four-choice questions, 57 subjects, 4 domains
\hfill{\small\color{mgray}(questions $\to$ subjects $\to$ domains)}

\vspace{1em}
\textbf{Two models:}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{GPT-4o-mini} (OpenAI API, \texttt{top\_logprobs=20})
  \item \textbf{Llama-3-8B-Instruct} (Colab T4, 4-bit quantization)
\end{itemize}

\vspace{1em}
\textbf{Confidence measure} — renormalized softmax over the four answer tokens:
\[
  c_i = \max_{a \in \{A,B,C,D\}} \frac{e^{\ell_a}}{\sum_{a'} e^{\ell_{a'}}}
\]

\vspace{0.5em}
\textbf{Primary outcome} — signed calibration gap:
\[
  \mathrm{gap}_i = c_i - y_i \quad (y_i \in \{0,1\})
\]
Positive = overconfident; negative = underconfident.
\end{frame}
\note{
  \textbf{[~45 sec]}

  Explain the hierarchy briefly: ``MMLU has 57 subjects like clinical medicine,
  formal logic, high school math — grouped into 4 broad domains. That nested structure
  is what makes a multilevel model the right tool.''

  For the confidence measure: ``We extract the log probability the model assigns to
  each of the four answer tokens, renormalize them to sum to 1, and take the max.
  This is a standard approach — it gives us a proper probability on the model's
  chosen answer.''

  For the signed gap: ``The nice thing about using the signed gap instead of ECE is
  that it tells us the \emph{direction} — positive means the model thinks it knows
  more than it does.''

  One methodological note if it comes up: we had to use top\_logprobs=20 for GPT
  because with 5 logprobs, 55\% of questions had at least one answer token missing,
  which would artificially inflate confidence estimates. That's a real data quality
  issue that took some debugging.

  \textbf{[If asked: what exactly is the signed calibration gap?]}
  The gap is computed at the \emph{question level}: for each of the 14,042 questions,
  we take the model's renormalized confidence $c_i \in (0,1]$ (the max softmax
  probability over A/B/C/D) and subtract the binary correctness indicator $y_i \in \{0,1\}$.
  So $\mathrm{gap}_i = c_i - y_i$ is a number in $(-1, 1)$ for every individual question.
  It is positive when the model was more confident than it deserved (overconfident)
  and negative when it was less confident than it deserved (underconfident).

  From that question-level quantity we derive everything else:
  \begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Multilevel regression}: all 14,042 gap values are the outcome.
      The intercept (0.196) is the grand mean; the fixed effects (word count, entropy)
      are slopes; the random effects partition variance across subjects and domains.
    \item \textbf{Subject mean gap} (bar chart, Finding 3): simple average of
      $\mathrm{gap}_i$ across all questions in a subject. A subject with mean gap
      $= 0.487$ (moral scenarios) means that on average the model was 49 percentage
      points more confident than it was accurate.
    \item \textbf{BH testing}: for each subject, a one-sample $t$-test on the
      question-level gaps tests whether the mean gap is significantly different
      from zero.
  \end{itemize}
  We do \emph{not} bin the gap — ECE does the binning. The gap is a raw question-level
  residual; ECE is a post-hoc aggregation that throws away sign and is sensitive to
  bin count. That is precisely why we use gap for regression and ECE only for ranking.

  \textbf{[If asked: signed gap vs.\ ECE]}
  We use both, but for different purposes. The signed gap is our question-level outcome
  for the multilevel regression — it preserves direction and supports standard inference.
  ECE is a subject-level scalar used for calibration curves and cross-model rankings;
  it cannot be used as a regression outcome because it is already aggregated.
  When miscalibration is uniformly in one direction (as it is here), the mean signed
  gap and ECE are closely related: ECE $\approx$ $|$mean gap$|$ plus a within-subject
  variation term. Both tell the same story; they serve different analytical roles.

  \textbf{[If asked: why only uppercase A, B, C, D?]}
  For GPT-4o-mini, the prompt explicitly instructs ``Answer with a single letter:
  A, B, C, or D'' and ends with ``Answer:'' — instruction-tuned models follow this
  reliably. With top\_logprobs=20, any token in the top 20 is captured; if a lowercase
  letter appeared and the uppercase did not, the uppercase would receive a default of
  $-100$ (essentially zero probability). In practice this is rare given the explicit
  prompt constraint.
  For Llama, we extract logits over the \emph{full vocabulary} at the generation
  position and directly index the token IDs for ``\textvisiblespace A'',
  ``\textvisiblespace B'', ``\textvisiblespace C'', ``\textvisiblespace D''
  (space-prefixed, as they appear naturally after ``The answer is'').
  This is a direct lookup, not a search — we get the exact softmax probability at
  those positions regardless of what other tokens were likely.
  Lowercase letters have different token IDs and are not summed in; they are simply
  excluded from the renormalization. This is standard in the MMLU logprob literature
  \citep{kadavath2022language} and is unlikely to bias results since the prefix
  strongly primes uppercase completions.

  \textbf{[If asked: why is 4-bit quantization acceptable?]}
  Running Llama-3-8B-Instruct in full precision (bfloat16) requires $\sim$16\,GB of
  GPU memory, which exceeds a Colab T4 (15\,GB). 4-bit NF4 quantization via
  BitsAndBytes reduces this to $\sim$5\,GB with minimal accuracy loss — the QLoRA
  paper (Dettmers et al.\ 2023) reports $<$1--2\% accuracy degradation across
  standard benchmarks. Our observed accuracy of 60.6\% vs.\ the reported $\sim$64\%
  for full-precision Llama-3-8B-Instruct is consistent with this range.
  Crucially, for calibration heterogeneity research, the \emph{relative} pattern
  across subjects matters more than absolute accuracy. The strongest evidence that
  quantization did not distort our findings is the cross-model Spearman $\rho = 0.846$:
  a heavily degraded model would not replicate GPT-4o-mini's subject-level rankings
  this faithfully.
}

\begin{frame}{Hierarchical model}
\begin{block}{Three-level mixed model}
  Questions ($i$) nested in subjects ($j$) nested in domains ($k$):
  \vspace{0.3em}
  \[
    \mathrm{gap}_{ijk} \;=\; \mu + \mathbf{x}_{ijk}^\top \boldsymbol{\beta}
      + u_k + v_{jk} + \varepsilon_{ijk}
  \]
\end{block}

\vspace{0.5em}
\begin{columns}[T]
  \begin{column}{0.48\textwidth}
    \textbf{Fixed effects}\\[0.3em]
    \small
    $\mu$: global intercept (mean overconfidence)\\[0.2em]
    $\boldsymbol{\beta}$: question-level covariates\\
    \quad (word count, entropy, negation,\\
    \quad max choice length) — all standardized
  \end{column}
  \begin{column}{0.48\textwidth}
    \textbf{Random effects}\\[0.3em]
    \small
    $u_k \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\;\sigma^2_{\text{domain}})$\\[0.2em]
    $v_{jk} \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\;\sigma^2_{\text{subject}})$\\[0.2em]
    $\varepsilon_{ijk} \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\;\sigma^2_\varepsilon)$\\[0.5em]
    Estimated via REML\\
    (\texttt{statsmodels.MixedLM})
  \end{column}
\end{columns}

\vspace{0.8em}
{\small\color{mgray}
  ICC decomposition: what fraction of calibration gap variance is between-subject vs.\ within-subject?}
\end{frame}
\note{
  \textbf{[~30 sec]}

  Walk through the model briefly: ``We model the signed calibration gap — confidence
  minus accuracy — at the question level, with random intercepts for both subject and
  domain.''

  Emphasize the key question: ``The ICC decomposition will tell us whether the
  miscalibration is driven by specific subjects, or whether it's spread evenly across
  all questions regardless of subject.''

  Mention REML: ``We use restricted maximum likelihood, which gives unbiased variance
  component estimates — important here since the variance decomposition is our main
  inferential target.''

  \textbf{[If asked: why fixed effects for questions and random effects for subject/domain?]}
  The core justification is about whether you have a theory worth committing to.

  At the \textbf{question level}, you have interpretable, measurable features and a
  plausible theory: longer questions are harder to reason about; higher entropy means
  the model is more uncertain; negations are known to trip up models. You can name
  the features, measure them, and interpret the coefficients. Fixed effects are the
  right tool when you have a theory.

  At the \textbf{subject and domain level}, you don't have a good theory of
  \emph{which} subject-level features drive miscalibration --- is it abstractness?
  normativity? rarity in pretraining data? Because you can't specify those features,
  you rely on mere group membership: ``I know subjects differ systematically, I'll
  let the data estimate how much, without committing to why.'' That is exactly what
  random effects do --- they capture the variance across groups without requiring you
  to name a mechanism.

  There is also a practical reason: fitting 57 subject fixed effects would burn
  degrees of freedom and overfit, especially for small subjects (some have only 100
  questions). Random effects pool information across subjects via the ICC, which is
  more efficient.

  One caveat: the domain random effect is barely identified with only 4 groups, which
  is exactly why it collapses to zero. Be upfront about this if asked --- it is a
  structural limitation of MMLU's 4-domain design, not a modeling failure.
}

\begin{columns}[T]
  \begin{column}{0.52\textwidth}
    \begin{table}
      \small
      \caption{Variance decomposition (both models)}
      \begin{tabular}{lrrrr}

        \bottomrule
      \end{tabular}
    \end{table}

    \vspace{0.3em}
    \small
    Both models: between-subject structure exists but is modest.\\[0.3em]
    GPT: \textbf{7.9\%} between-subject ICC.\\
    Llama: \textbf{2.0\%} — more diffuse miscalibration.
  \end{column}
  \begin{column}{0.44\textwidth}
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/domain_ece_2.png}
    \small\color{mgray} Domain-level mean ECE (descriptive)
  \end{column}
\end{columns}
\end{frame}
\note{
  \textbf{[~30 sec]}

  Lead with the structure: ``Both models show that the vast majority of calibration
  variance — 92 to 98\% — is at the question level. But a meaningful slice is
  systematically concentrated at the subject level. That's not noise — it's structure.''

  Highlight the difference: ``GPT shows 7.9\% between-subject ICC, Llama shows 2.0\%.
  Llama's miscalibration is more diffuse — spread more evenly across subjects rather
  than concentrated in particular domains.''

  \textbf{[Important: what ICC does and does not say]}
  ICC = 7.9\% does NOT mean calibration barely varies across subjects.
  It means 7.9\% of total question-level variance is attributable to subject membership.
  That 7.9\% is still large enough to produce a 13$\times$ range in mean subject gaps
  (0.037 to 0.487) --- the two facts are compatible.
  Most question-to-question variability is noise regardless of subject (the 92\%),
  but the systematic component that does exist is substantively meaningful.

  Be upfront about domain ICC = 0: ``Domain-level variance is estimated at zero.
  That doesn't mean domains don't matter descriptively — we see clear differences in
  the bar chart. But the model can't separate domain-level variance from subject-level
  variance because the subject random effects absorb whatever between-domain structure
  exists. With only 4 domain groups we also have very low power to detect a non-zero
  domain component even if one were present.''

  \textbf{[If asked: how do we get the variance components and ICC?]}
  Fitting the mixed model via REML directly outputs the variance components
  $\sigma^2_\text{domain}$, $\sigma^2_\text{subject}$, and $\sigma^2_\text{residual}$
  as part of the optimization — no separate step needed. REML is preferred over
  standard MLE because MLE tends to underestimate variance components (it doesn't
  account for the degrees of freedom consumed by the fixed effects); REML corrects
  for this and gives unbiased estimates.

  The ICC is then just arithmetic: divide each variance component by the total.
  For GPT-4o-mini: total variance $= 0.000 + 0.013 + 0.150 = 0.163$, so
  $\text{ICC}_\text{subject} = 0.013 / 0.163 = 7.9\%$.
  The ICC answers: of all the variance in the calibration gap, what fraction is
  explained by knowing which subject a question comes from?
  7.9\% means subject identity accounts for a meaningful but modest share ---
  some subjects are persistently harder to calibrate on, but most of the
  variation is question-level noise that subject alone can't predict.
}

\begin{frame}{Finding 2: all 57 subjects are overconfident on average}
  \begin{center}
    \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{figures/top5_miscalibrated_gpt4o.pdf}
  \end{center}
  \vspace{-0.5em}
  {\small The 5 most miscalibrated subjects shown. All 57 subjects have positive mean
  calibration gap. In the high-confidence region where data is concentrated, curves
  lie below the diagonal throughout. Isotonic (solid) and kernel-smoothed (dashed)
  curves agree where support exists; divergence at low confidence reflects empty bins.}
\end{frame}
\note{
  \textbf{[~30 sec]}

  Lead with the universal claim carefully: ``All 57 subjects have a positive mean
  calibration gap — they are overconfident on average. These 5 curves show what
  that looks like in the region where data is actually concentrated, which for most
  subjects is the high-confidence end.''

  Be precise: the below-diagonal pattern holds in the region of support (roughly
  0.85--1.0 for most subjects). It is NOT correct to say every point on every curve
  is below the diagonal — 23/57 subjects have parts of their isotonic curve above
  the diagonal, but this is at low-confidence bins where there are essentially no
  data points. The mean gap being positive is the correct universal claim.

  On the formal\_logic kernel curve specifically: the dashed curve reads near 1.0
  from confidence 0.03 to 0.53. This is a smoothing artifact — formal\_logic has
  zero questions in those bins (126 total questions, 89 of which are in the
  0.95--1.00 bin). The kernel is smoothing over empty space and should not be
  interpreted there. The isotonic curve is more honest: it just holds the last
  observed value flat across empty bins.

  Point out isotonic vs.\ kernel agreement where it matters: ``In the region where
  data exists — above 0.7 or so — the solid and dashed lines track each other,
  which validates the monotonicity assumption in that range.''

  Transition to Finding 3: ``The next slide shows the full picture across all 57 —
  the key result is not just that they are all overconfident on average, but that
  the magnitude varies 13-fold.''

  \textbf{[If asked: what does `5 most miscalibrated' mean — ECE or signed gap?]}
  These are the 5 subjects with the highest ECE, computed per subject as the weighted
  mean absolute deviation between the reliability curve and the diagonal across 20 bins.
  ECE is always positive — it measures magnitude but not direction.
  Since all 57 subjects have positive mean gap (universally overconfident), the top 5
  by ECE and top 5 by mean gap are essentially the same ranking here. But they are
  conceptually different: if some subjects were underconfident, ECE would still flag
  them as miscalibrated while the signed gap would show negative values.

  \textbf{[If asked: what does below vs.\ above the diagonal mean?]}
  Below the diagonal = overconfident: the model says 0.9 but is right only 0.5 of
  the time. Users trust it more than they should.
  Above the diagonal = underconfident: the model says 0.6 but is right 0.8 of the
  time. It hedges more than necessary.
  We find universal overconfidence — all curves below the diagonal — which is
  consistent with RLHF fine-tuning pushing probability mass toward the chosen answer
  to produce confident, helpful-sounding responses. Base models typically show
  better calibration \citep{kadavath2022language, nakkiran2025trained}.
  Overconfidence is the worse deployment failure: an underconfident model prompts
  users to verify; an overconfident model in legal or medical settings actively
  misleads users who treat confidence as a reliability signal.

  \textbf{[Limitation: support is concentrated at high confidence]}
  The confidence distribution is extremely right-skewed — the median confidence
  across all questions is 1.000 and 90\% of questions have confidence above 0.85.
  This is subject-dependent: math subjects (e.g., high school mathematics, abstract
  algebra) show real spread down to 0.25, so their curves are informative across a
  wider range. But for most subjects — including virology and formal logic in this
  plot — confidence is concentrated near 1.0, meaning the left portion of the
  reliability curve has essentially no data behind it and should not be interpreted.
  The curves characterize high-confidence behavior well but tell us little about
  how the model behaves when it expresses uncertainty, because it almost never does.
}

      \vspace{0.8em}

      \begin{itemize}

      \end{itemize}

      Humanities subjects cluster at the top; STEM and ``Other'' at the bottom.

      \vspace{0.8em}
      {\color{mgray}\small Intercept $= 0.196$ ($p < 0.001$); overconfidence is a

    \end{column}
  \end{columns}
\end{frame}
\note{
  \textbf{[~45 sec]}

  Preempt the obvious question: ``57/57 rejected sounds impressive, but it's largely
  a consequence of large sample size and strong global overconfidence — not a deep
  finding. The interesting result is the 13-fold range in magnitude.''

  \textbf{What is the 13$\times$?} Simply the ratio of the largest to smallest mean gap:
  moral scenarios (0.487) divided by marketing (0.037) $\approx 13\times$.
  The model is 13 times more overconfident on moral scenarios than on marketing.
  That heterogeneity is invisible in any single aggregate ECE number.

  ``A gap of 0.487 means the model is, on average, nearly 50 percentage points more
  confident than it is accurate. A gap of 0.037 means it is essentially well-calibrated.
  Those are qualitatively different deployment situations.''

  \textbf{[If asked: why is 57/57 not a meaningful finding?]}
  Intuition: imagine testing whether a coin is slightly biased. With 10 flips you
  might not detect it. With 1{,}000{,}000 flips you would detect even a 50.001\%
  bias as ``statistically significant'' — but that doesn't mean it matters.
  Same here: with 14{,}042 questions and a model already globally overconfident by
  0.196 on average, you have enough power to flag even a trivially small true effect.
  Even marketing (gap $= 0.037$, the best subject) gets rejected because the sample
  is large enough to detect anything nonzero.

  \textbf{Where does BH fit in?} BH controls the false discovery rate — it adjusts
  the rejection threshold to limit false positives across 57 simultaneous tests.
  But the problem here is not false positives; it's that all 57 true effects are
  real but some are scientifically trivial. BH can prevent you from rejecting nulls
  that aren't true; it cannot prevent you from correctly rejecting nulls that aren't
  interesting. When all 57 p-values are essentially zero, BH has nothing to correct.

  The analogy: testing whether 57 cities are warmer than absolute zero. Technically
  all 57 reject, BH agrees. But the interesting question is which cities are warm
  enough to matter. That's what the magnitude range answers.
}

\begin{frame}{What predicts the gap? Fixed effects}
  \begin{columns}[T]
    \begin{column}{0.48\textwidth}
      \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/coef_plot_2.png}
    \end{column}
    \begin{column}{0.48\textwidth}
      \small
      \textbf{GPT-4o-mini:}
      \begin{itemize}
        \item Word count ($p < 0.001$): longer $\Rightarrow$ larger gap
        \item Entropy ($p < 0.001$): more uncertainty $\Rightarrow$ larger gap
      \end{itemize}

      \vspace{0.5em}
      \textbf{Llama-3-8B-Instruct:}
      \begin{itemize}
        \item No significant predictors ($p > 0.05$)
        \item Entropy marginally positive ($p = 0.069$) — same direction
      \end{itemize}

      \vspace{0.5em}
      \small\color{mgray}
      Weaker predictor signal for Llama is consistent with lower ICC (2.0\%):
      less between-subject structure $\Rightarrow$ less to predict.
    \end{column}
  \end{columns}
\end{frame}
\note{
  \textbf{[~30 sec]}

  Lead with the GPT entropy finding — it's counterintuitive: ``When GPT-4o-mini
  spreads its probability mass across options, expressing uncertainty, the gap
  actually gets larger. The model isn't reliably correct more often when it's
  hedging; it's miscalibrated even in its expression of uncertainty.''

  Then contrast with Llama: ``Llama shows no significant predictors. The trend
  for entropy is in the same direction but doesn't reach significance. This is
  consistent with Llama having less between-subject structure overall — there's
  simply less systematic variance to explain.''

  \textbf{[If asked: why do GPT and Llama differ in fixed effect significance?]}
  GPT's ICC is 7.9\% --- its miscalibration is organized by subject, concentrating
  in subjects like abstract algebra and moral scenarios. These subjects also tend to
  have longer, more ambiguous questions. So word count and entropy are picking up
  real signal because they correlate with the hard subjects.
  Llama's ICC is only 2.0\% --- its miscalibration is more diffuse, not concentrated
  in particular subjects. There is no such structure for question features to exploit.
  On top of that, Llama's residual variance is larger in absolute terms
  ($\sigma^2_\varepsilon = 0.190$ vs.\ $0.150$ for GPT), so the same fixed effects
  face more noise, inflating standard errors and making significance harder to reach.

  \textbf{[If pushed on the fixed effects interpretation --- important limitation]}
  The question-level predictors (word count, entropy) are standardized across the
  full dataset, not within subjects. This means the fixed effect estimates blend two
  things that should ideally be separated:
  \begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Within-subject effect}: within abstract algebra, do longer questions
      get larger gaps?
    \item \textbf{Between-subject effect}: do subjects whose questions tend to be
      longer (like abstract algebra) also tend to have larger gaps?
  \end{itemize}
  Subject random intercepts absorb the between-subject \emph{baseline} in the gap,
  but they do not remove the between-subject covariation between the predictors and
  the gap. The clean fix is \textbf{group-mean centering}: subtract each subject's
  mean word count from each question's word count before fitting. That isolates the
  pure within-subject effect. Without it, the fixed effects are dataset-level
  associations, not clean question-level effects.

  The honest answer if pressed: ``The fixed effects should be interpreted as
  associations between question features and the calibration gap, not as cleanly
  isolated question-level effects. This is a real limitation of RQ2. The primary
  findings --- ICC decomposition, the 13$\times$ magnitude range, and the
  cross-model $\rho = 0.846$ --- do not depend on the fixed effects at all and
  are unaffected by this.''
}

\begin{frame}{Cross-model agreement: miscalibration is task-driven}
  \begin{columns}[T]
    \begin{column}{0.48\textwidth}

\note{
  \textbf{[~45 sec]}

  \textbf{[Big picture: how this slide connects to earlier findings]}
  Three separate claims have been made so far --- it is easy to conflate them:
  \begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Universal overconfidence} (global intercept $= 0.196$): all 57
      subjects are overconfident because the model itself is globally biased by RLHF.
      The 57/57 BH rejection is mostly detecting this global effect, not between-subject
      differences.
    \item \textbf{Between-subject heterogeneity in magnitude} (ICC $= 7.9\%$, 13$\times$
      range): on top of the global bias, subjects vary in how badly miscalibrated they
      are. 7.9\% of total variance is between-subject --- modest, but enough to produce
      a 13$\times$ range in mean gaps that matters practically.
    \item \textbf{Task-driven miscalibration} (this slide, $\rho = 0.846$): whatever
      between-subject variation exists is consistent across two very different models.
      This answers a different question than ICC --- not ``how much variation is there?''
      but ``where does that variation come from?''
  \end{enumerate}
  The cross-model result does not contradict the low ICC. It says: the subject-level
  structure that does exist ($7.9\%$) is driven by the task, not the model.

  \textbf{[What does Spearman $\rho = 0.846$ exactly say?]}
  Spearman rank correlation ranks all 57 subjects by ECE separately for each model,
  then measures how well those two orderings agree. $\rho = 0.846$ means the rankings
  are strongly correlated --- subjects GPT ranks as most miscalibrated, Llama also
  tends to rank near the top, and vice versa. It is a rank correlation (not Pearson),
  so it is robust to outliers and non-linear scaling differences between the two models
  --- it only asks ``do the orderings agree?'', not ``are the absolute ECE values the same?''
  $\rho = 1.0$ would be perfect rank agreement; $\rho = 0$ would mean no relationship.
  $0.846$ is strong. The bootstrap 95\% CI $[0.734, 0.910]$ confirms this is not a
  fluke driven by a few extreme subjects.

  \textbf{What does it imply?} If miscalibration were model-specific --- a quirk of
  GPT's RLHF or Llama's supervised fine-tuning --- you would expect low correlation:
  each model would have its own idiosyncratic hard subjects. The high $\rho$ means
  the hardness is a property of the knowledge domain itself. Combined with 57/57
  overlap, there is strong evidence that no subject escapes miscalibration and that
  the ranking of which subjects are worst is determined by the task, not the model.

  This is a strong result — emphasize it: ``Both a large proprietary model fine-tuned
  with RLHF and a small open-weights model fine-tuned with supervised learning produce
  essentially the same pattern. The Spearman correlation of 0.846 means subject
  rankings are highly consistent.''

  The key takeaway: ``This is not a GPT problem or a Llama problem. It's a knowledge
  domain problem. The subjects that are hard to calibrate on are hard for both models we tested.
  That means recalibration needs to happen at the subject level,
  not the model level.''

  Point to the scatter plot: ``Every dot is red — both models reject. The dots above
  the diagonal are subjects where Llama is worse; below are where GPT is worse. But
  both are bad.''

  \textbf{[If asked: what can and cannot be concluded from $\rho = 0.846$?]}
  \textbf{Can conclude:}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item The subject rankings are highly consistent across models --- what GPT finds
      hardest, Llama also finds hardest.
    \item Miscalibration difficulty is a property of the knowledge domain, not of
      how either model was trained. Model-specific fixes (retraining, better RLHF)
      are unlikely to resolve it.
  \end{itemize}
  \textbf{Cannot conclude:}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item That absolute ECE values are the same --- Llama could be worse overall
      while still ranking subjects identically. Spearman only compares orderings.
    \item That this generalizes beyond these two models --- two models is suggestive
      but not definitive. A broader comparison across families and scales would
      strengthen the claim.
    \item \emph{Why} certain subjects are harder --- whether it is abstractness of
      reasoning, rarity in pretraining data, or something else is an open question.
  \end{itemize}

  \textbf{[If asked: what does `recalibration at the subject level' mean?]}
  The standard recalibration method is \textbf{temperature scaling}: fit a single
  scalar $T$ that divides all logits, shifting confidence up or down globally.
  This is a model-level fix --- one parameter applied to every question regardless
  of subject.

  The problem: with a 13$\times$ range in gap magnitude, a single $T$ cannot work
  for all subjects simultaneously. A temperature calibrated to fix moral scenarios
  (gap $= 0.487$) would massively overcorrect marketing (gap $= 0.037$), making it
  underconfident. A temperature appropriate for the average subject leaves moral
  scenarios still badly miscalibrated.

  \textbf{Subject-level recalibration} means fitting a separate temperature per
  subject: $T_{\text{moral\_scenarios}}$, $T_{\text{marketing}}$, etc. When the
  model answers a question, apply the temperature for that subject. This directly
  addresses the heterogeneity.

  \textbf{Why does $\rho = 0.846$ matter for this argument?} Because both GPT and
  Llama rank the same subjects as hardest, the miscalibration is a property of the
  knowledge domain itself --- something about moral reasoning or abstract algebra
  is inherently hard to calibrate on, regardless of model architecture or training
  procedure. Even a much better future model would likely show the same hard subjects.
  The fix therefore needs to be applied at deployment time at the subject level,
  not addressed by retraining the model.
}

\textbf{Three stress tests — run on both models.}

    Llama: 55/57/57 rejected\\[0.3em]

  \end{column}
  \begin{column}{0.32\textwidth}

    \small
    3-level vs.\ 2-level model.\\[0.3em]
    GPT: ICC$_\text{subj}=0.079$\\
    Llama: ICC$_\text{subj}=0.020$\\
    (identical in both specs)\\[0.3em]
    \textbf{ICC robust.}
  \end{column}
\end{columns}
\end{frame}
\note{
  \textbf{[~45 sec]}

  Frame briefly: ``Before trusting these results, I stress-tested the three main
  modeling choices across both models.''

  Column 1: ``Subject rankings don't move when you change the number of ECE bins,
  and they correlate strongly ($\rho \ge 0.861$) with the unbinned Negative
  Log Calibration Score (NLCS). Our findings aren't artifacts of binning choices.''

  Column 2: ``At alpha=0.01, GPT still rejects 56/57 and Llama rejects 55/57. The
  two Llama subjects that drop out at the strictest threshold have adjusted p-values
  of 0.014 and 0.031 — just above the cutoff. The finding is not alpha-sensitive.''

  \textbf{[If asked: what does varying the BH $\alpha$ tell us?]}
  BH $\alpha$ controls the expected proportion of false discoveries among all rejected
  tests. At $\alpha = 0.05$, at most 5\% of rejections are expected to be false;
  at $\alpha = 0.01$, at most 1\%.

  If rejections were borderline --- p-values clustered just below the threshold ---
  tightening $\alpha$ would flip many decisions. The fact that results barely move
  (57/57 $\to$ 56/57 for GPT, 57/57 $\to$ 55/57 for Llama) shows almost all subjects
  have adjusted p-values so far below any reasonable threshold that the BH correction
  barely matters. The two or three subjects that drop at $\alpha = 0.01$ have adjusted
  p-values of 0.014 and 0.031 --- just above the cutoff, not strongly non-significant.

  Someone cannot dismiss the finding by saying ``you chose a lenient $\alpha$'' ---
  at the strictest threshold tested you still reject 55--56 of 57 subjects.

  This also reinforces the earlier point that 57/57 is not a deep finding: p-values
  are near zero for most subjects because the global intercept (0.196) is large
  relative to per-subject standard errors. BH has almost nothing to correct because
  the evidence is overwhelming regardless of $\alpha$. The sensitivity check confirms
  robustness, but the interesting result remains the 13$\times$ magnitude range,
  not the binary rejection count.

  Column 3: ``Collapsing the domain level doesn't change the ICC estimates at all —
  because domain variance is zero anyway, the two-level and three-level models are
  numerically identical.''

  \textbf{[If asked: why Spearman specifically for the bin sensitivity check?]}
  Our conclusions rest on subject \emph{rankings}, not absolute ECE values:
  the top-5 miscalibrated subjects are selected by rank, and the cross-model
  comparison ($\rho = 0.846$) is itself a rank correlation. When we change bin
  count, absolute ECE values do shift slightly (mean ECE goes from 0.190 at
  10 bins to 0.194 at 30 bins) --- but that is expected and irrelevant.
  What matters is whether the ordering of subjects changes.
  Spearman $\rho$ directly measures rank agreement and ignores absolute scale
  differences. Pearson would penalize the harmless shift in absolute values.
  $\rho \geq 0.997$ for GPT means: no matter which bin count you choose between
  10 and 30, the subject ordering is essentially identical.
  The same logic applies to the NLCS comparison: we are not asking whether NLCS
  equals ECE numerically, but whether they agree on which subjects are worst.
  $\rho \geq 0.861$ says they do.
}

\begin{frame}{Takeaways}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{The unit of analysis is the subject, not the model.}
    Aggregate ECE hides a 13$\times$ range in miscalibration magnitude.
    57/57 subjects rejected is the wrong headline --- the magnitude variation is what matters.

  \vspace{0.4em}
  \item \textbf{Miscalibration is task-driven, so subject-level fixes are needed.}
    Spearman $\rho = 0.846$ across two very different models means the same subjects
    are hardest regardless of architecture or training.
    Global temperature scaling cannot fix subject-specific structure.

  \vspace{0.4em}
  \item \textbf{The audit framework generalizes.}
    Multilevel model + BH correction + shrinkage applies to any benchmark with
    a hierarchical structure --- a reusable tool for honest calibration evaluation.

  \vspace{0.4em}
  \item \textbf{Open questions remain.}
    Two models is suggestive, not definitive. We can describe \emph{which} subjects
    are hard but not \emph{why} --- abstractness, training data rarity, or something
    else is still unknown.
\end{enumerate}

\vspace{0.4em}
\textbf{Next steps:} subject-specific temperature scaling; broader cross-model
comparison; extend to open-ended generation.

\end{frame}
